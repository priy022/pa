HealthCo · PA Intelligence Framework

Prior authorization is broken.
Here is how to fix the structure.

PA fails not because teams execute poorly — it fails because the process has no memory. The data is unambiguous. The burden is systemic. And the standard responses don't address the root cause. This framework does.

The scale of the problem

The human cost is documented, not hypothesized. Among physicians surveyed by the AMA: 19% report a PA led to a patient being hospitalized, 13% report life-threatening events or permanent impairment, and 7% report patient disability, permanent damage, or death. Among patients who experienced a denial or delay, one in three say it had a major negative impact on their mental health. One in four say it damaged their physical health. Prior authorization is not an administrative nuisance. It is a patient safety issue.

Why existing responses don't fix it

The standard playbook — train staff, submit earlier, outsource to a third-party vendor — treats PA as an execution problem. It isn't. These interventions reduce friction at the margins. None of them address the structural failure.

Train staff better
Works until the coordinator leaves. Payer knowledge is carried in their head, not encoded in the process. When they go, the practice relearns the same lessons. The AMA found practices average 43 PAs per physician per week — at that volume, institutional memory is not a scalable strategy.
Submit earlier
Addresses timing, not quality. A plan that doesn't meet payer standards submitted two weeks early is still a denial. And submitting earlier doesn't help when payers change requirements without notice — which clinical research confirms they do, frequently and without announcement.
Third-party vendors
Speed up the workflow without learning from it. They submit faster versions of the same packages that got denied before. Current AI-assisted PA tools help with member details and eligibility — not with the pattern intelligence that prevents denials before they happen.
Peer-to-peer review
Necessary but structurally broken. Only 15% of physicians report peer-to-peer consultations are with a clinician who has appropriate qualifications. It is a reactive tool at the end of a failed process — not a fix for the process itself.
The structural diagnosis

The process has no memory. Every denial contains a signal — which payer enforces which rule, which documentation gaps trigger which flags, which provider patterns predict which failures. That signal exists in every case that closes. It just isn't being captured, categorized, and used to prevent the next failure. The problem isn't execution speed. It's architecture.

Structural failure 1
Payer rules are learned through denials
Requirements aren't published clearly. The practice learns what a payer requires by getting denied for not meeting it. That knowledge never makes it into a system — it lives in one coordinator's head until they leave.
Structural failure 2
Provider patterns go undetected
Some providers consistently submit plans with the same documentation gaps. Without pattern detection across submissions, those gaps surface one case at a time — as denials, not as coaching interventions before the next submission goes out.
Structural failure 3
Payer behavior shifts go unnoticed
Payers change enforcement without announcement. Drug restrictions in Medicare Part D rose from 31.9% in 2011 to 44.4% by 2020. A rule that passed for years can fail overnight — and the practice only learns about it after enough denials confirm the shift.
The framework — a process that addresses the structural failures

Four phases. Three actors. One learning loop. Each phase is designed to address a specific friction point in the current system — not to execute faster, but to build a process that compounds with every case.

HealthCo · Prior Authorization Operational Flow · A Learning System That Improves With Every Case
Phase 01
Pre-submission gate
Clinician + AI chief of staff
Addresses: denials from known, preventable failures
  • AI chief of staff runs two checks — are required documents present, and do those documents actually meet payer standards?
  • Hard blocks stop the submission. Soft flags go to the coordinator with context: not just what's flagged, but why, and how often that specific issue has caused a denial with this payer.
  • Loop repeats until both checks pass — or the coordinator overrides and logs the reason. Every override is signal.
  • What this fixes: payer-specific requirements that were previously learned through denials are encoded once and protect every future submission permanently.
Phase 02
Submission & monitoring
Coordinator + Payer
Addresses: unmanaged auth windows and silent pend failures
  • Coordinator reviews the AI assessment — role is to validate, not rediscover. The case arrives pre-analyzed.
  • Active monitoring: Day 15 confirm receipt · Day 30 check status · Day 45 escalate if no decision
  • Auth expiry check: if pending window overlaps current auth expiry, continuity of care alert fires automatically before a service gap occurs
  • What this fixes: 29% of insured adults report delayed access to care. Active monitoring prevents silent pend failures from becoming service lapses and revenue gaps.
Phase 03
Outcome handling
Clinician + Coordinator + AI
Addresses: denials that recur because outcomes aren't categorized
  • Every non-approval generates a categorized signal: clinical, administrative, or payer rule — each type requires a different response and a different learning destination
  • Clinical failure → clinician revision · Administrative failure → coordinator corrects · Payer rule → knowledge base updated, prevents recurrence on all future submissions to that payer
  • Denials trigger peer-to-peer preparation: AI chief of staff builds structured talking points. Only 15% of P2P calls are with an appropriately qualified reviewer — preparation quality is the only lever the practice controls.
  • What this fixes: outcomes that previously disappeared into closed cases now feed directly back into the system.
Phase 04
Continuous learning
AI chief of staff + Ops
Addresses: the core structural failure — a process with no memory
  • All signals — denials, ADRs, revisions, overrides — captured, tagged by payer, provider, and failure type, evaluated continuously
  • Pattern threshold: same flag in >40% of a provider's last 10 submissions within 90 days → coaching alert to ops lead before the next denial
  • Payer intelligence: when a payer's enforcement pattern shifts, the system surfaces it before the denial rate moves — not after enough cases accumulate to confirm it retrospectively
  • Rule updates feed back into Phase 01: stronger hard blocks, refined soft flags, updated confidence scores
  • What this fixes: payer enforcement shifts that previously went undetected for months are surfaced before they generate a denial trend.
The right role for AI — and the wrong one

AI is entering PA on both sides. Payers use it to manage denials. Providers use it to manage submissions. The architecture of the AI layer determines whether it helps or causes harm.

The cautionary case: UnitedHealthcare's nH Predict tool reportedly had a 90% error rate, denying medically necessary care to thousands of elderly and disabled Medicare beneficiaries — leading to a federal class action lawsuit. The failure wasn't that AI was used. It was that AI made decisions without meaningful human oversight. The lesson is precision about what the AI does and doesn't do.

What the AI chief of staff does
Assesses every submission against a structured knowledge base of payer requirements. Flags hard blocks and soft risks. Detects provider patterns. Surfaces payer behavioral shifts. Generates peer-to-peer preparation from case records. Produces provider performance summaries and denial pattern reports.
What it never does
Make a submission decision. The coordinator reviews every AI assessment and owns every decision. Every override is logged. Every disagreement between the AI and the coordinator is signal — either the AI is miscalibrated, or the coordinator has knowledge the system needs to learn. Both are valuable. Neither is invisible.
Why the governance layer is non-negotiable
CMS-0057-F (January 2024) requires Medicare Advantage, Medicaid, and CHIP plans to support electronic PA with specific denial reasons and public metrics — but doesn't regulate how AI decisions are made. The governance architecture exists because an AI system without human oversight degrades silently and at scale.
The compounding advantage
Day one: structured QA layer. Month twelve: a proprietary knowledge base about how specific payers actually behave that no third-party vendor can replicate — built from the practice's own case data. The value appreciates with every submission processed.
How we measure — knowing whether the structural failures are actually being addressed

The one number: first-pass approval rate. Everything else explains why it moved.

The diagnostic: if clinical and payer-rule denial rates decline faster than administrative failures, the system is functioning as a learning system. If administrative failures decline fastest, it's a better checklist — valuable, but not what was built.

Honest limitation

The system cannot distinguish between a genuine FPR improvement and an easier case mix. The coordinator makes that call. FPR is the right headline — the category breakdown (clinical vs. admin vs. payer-rule denial trends) is what tells you whether improvement is real and durable.
